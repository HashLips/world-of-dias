# First Day in Veloria

## Premise

A newcomer enters Veloria City for the first time and learns, in a single day, that Dias is less a map and more a layered agreement between memory, frequency, and daily practice.

## Story

By sunrise, the road to Veloria was already crowded.

Carts leaned under produce, brass parts, and folded cloth; walkers moved in clusters and singles, everyone keeping enough lane for strangers to pass without argument. At the DEB Gate, a woman in travel-gray touched the stone frame with two fingers before stepping through, as if greeting an old host.

"First time?" she asked me.

I nodded.

"Then look up before you look in."

Above the city, the Sky Drifters moved like pale figures cut from cloud-light, too high to name and too steady to dismiss. Nobody screamed. Nobody ran. A boy beside me pointed once, then returned to counting copper marks into his mother's palm.

Inside the first ring, Veloria felt older than my grandmother's stories: stonework, hand tools, market bells, thick bread smoke. By midday, one ring inward, the streets changed shape and rhythm. Glass fronts. Cleaner lines. Faster talk. By late afternoon in the third ring, I passed service drones carrying crate-stacks between towers while a street singer performed a centuries-old tide song to a crowd that filmed her on wrist-screens.

"Same city," a porter said, seeing me stare. "Different layer of it."

Near evening, the outer light went gold over the basin and every surface seemed briefly polished from within. Even the paving seams held a warm line, thin as thread. People kept walking, but slower, like they all recognized the same minute and did not want to bruise it.

In a tea stand queue, two merchants argued over payment rates, then settled by converting to VEL marks at the posted board without insult or delay. No one celebrated. No one sulked. Trade moved on.

When darkness came, the core towers brightened.

From a ridge lane I could see the innermost district: compact, severe, and radiant. It did not glow like festival light; it pulsed like work in progress. Someone beside me said the Valorian Operators would be deep in shift by now, balancing loads most of us would never understand, keeping the city's heart from skipping.

"You thought Dias was one place?" the tea seller asked as she locked her window.

"I did this morning."

She smiled at that, not unkindly.

"It's one world. That is not the same thing."

I slept above a workshop in the second ring, and just before dawn I woke to a distant harmonic thrum that seemed to come from every direction at once.

On my first day in Veloria, nobody explained Dias to me.

They only lived it in front of me, layer by layer, until the idea became impossible to mistake.
