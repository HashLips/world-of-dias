# Lucky Fruit sharing-knife

## Overview

A **Lucky Fruit sharing-knife** is a small kitchen blade—often with a **pale wooden handle** and a **gentle curve**—used in Wabet-leaning and Nauw households when a **Lucky-looking fruit** (unusually even, unreasonably perfect) is served. The **custom** is the artifact: the knife is passed so the **first cut** is offered **outward**—to a guest, an elder, a child—**before** the house takes its own.

## Description

Blades are rarely ornate; the “luck” is in **use**, not inlay. Some families etch a **single line** (a horizon, a smile) to remind them the cut is **shared time**, not private treasure.

Nauw fruit reverence treatises sometimes joke that the true Lucky Fruit is **the moment of offering**, and the pips are only punctuation.

## Cultural Meaning

The knife encodes a **happy rule of thumb**: **sweetness multiplies in company** more reliably than in hoarding. It is a counterweight to competition myths around rare produce.

## Usage

Lore: domestic warmth, inter-household trust, and **comedy of manners** when someone forgets and tries to take the first slice themselves (usually forgiven with laughter).

## Notes

Does not require supernatural verification; the joy is **social**, not mechanistic.
