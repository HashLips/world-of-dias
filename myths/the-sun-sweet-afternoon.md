# The Sun-Sweet Afternoon

## Summary

A gentle **Velorian Basin** custom—half myth, half weather habit—says the first truly warm day of the year is **Sun-Sweet Afternoon**: a time when **work is allowed to be lighter**, and fruit sellers cut their **sun-sweet** samples a little larger, on purpose, without calling it charity.

## Myth Text

The story is not a miracle. It is a **permission** passed down: when the haze thins and the light turns honey-thick, you may walk slower. Apprentices are teased, not punished, for dawdling. Some guildhouses even ring a **single bell** not for work, but for *pause*.

Children are told: the heat is a guest; greet it with **water, shade, and a shared orange**. Lovers and friends repeat the line every year, sometimes laughing at how small it is—and still doing it.

In the most romantic tellings, the **basin** itself exhales, and the city rings remember they were built for **people** first.

## Notes

Intentionally **low-stakes, high-joy**—a relief valve in a region otherwise dense with **trade, rings, and pressure**. Pairs with fruit culture without naming conflict.
