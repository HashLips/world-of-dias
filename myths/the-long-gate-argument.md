# The Long Gate Argument

## Summary

A Nauw–Sorel tale describes the long southern crossing into Hearthvale not as a simple construction project, but as a protracted public argument with weight—negotiated between people who would later blame each other for the same bridge. It is as much a story about **who may say “yes” to open exile routes** as it is about stone and piers.

## Myth Text

They say the first plan was only a line on sand: a span from a Nauw south road to a Sorel bluff, straight as pride. The second plan was a dogleg, because a Velorian surveyor swore a straight line would "tear luck." The third was wider, the fourth was narrower, the fifth included a rest island nobody could afford.

Each revision held a different fear: that southern routes would **overfill** Nauw markets, that Sorel would send back plagues of opinion, that the Hearth would become a second capital, that it would not become a capital at all and therefore fail the hungry.

When the piers finally stood, neither side had won the story. Nauw called it a charity of commerce. Sorel called it a **gilded constraint** in polite clothing. Hearthvale—still half-settlement—called it a promise that could be walked.

Travelers on Brindle March still repeat: **the long gate is not a door; it is a threshold you walk while the scribes of two lands draw the same line once, finally, together.**

## Notes

Coordinates with practical geography: the great bridge is the main Nauw-to-northern-Sorel path through Hearthvale. The myth is **moral** more than **historical**; dates and names in local retellings do not always align.
