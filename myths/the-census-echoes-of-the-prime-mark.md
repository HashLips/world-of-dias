# The Census Echoes of the Prime Mark

## Summary

A Dias-spanning scholastic folktale claims the **Prime Realm** once kept a **single running tally** of all vibrational states—an impossible catalog of how **reality’s harmonics** could co-exist in one body. The tale is about **physics-of-being**, not the counting of people as property. The Fracture, in this version, is not only a break; it is **a scattered remainder** of that tally: “echoes of the mark” that survive as **Prime Relics**, half-legible notations, and the modern habit of naming frequency bands with numbers.

## Myth Text

They say a circle of arithmeticians and architects—sometimes called the **Census Ward**, sometimes only **the Tally**—inscribed a master column into the base of the Prime Bridge (or the bridge’s conceptual twin). Every band of existence had a mark; every mark had a place in the column. When the realm was whole, the column was **quiet**, because the world agreed with its own count.

The Fracture did not erase the numbers; it **pulled the column through itself**, like silk through a tear. The numbers reappeared as **echoes**—strange marks on old stones, **harmonic designations** that feel older than the cultures that use them, even **restless, list-making dreams** in scholars who get too near certain relic sites—**dreams of order**, not of dominion.

A softer ending claims the Tally is still completing itself across the Present Age, which is why new frequencies (and new names) can surface without anyone feeling the world has *ended* its expansion—only that it has been **re-balancing a sum**.

## Notes

Connects the **number-coded frequency bands** (F200, F432, F500) to a **mythic Prime bookkeeping of resonance** without locking canon to a single true mechanism, and without implying any in-world policy toward **unfree labor**—the Tally, where it is believed, concerns **vibration and category**, not persons as goods. Pairs with **Partial Harmonic Index fragment** as a low-grade “evidence” tradition without confirming the folktale as fact.
